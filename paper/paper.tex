\begin{abstract}
Life-like cellular automata exhibit \emph{local topological fragility:}
the initial density of weakly-connected live cells predicts future loss of
fine-scale connected components.
Across Conway's Game of Life (GoL) and HighLife, two grid sizes
($L\in\{64,128\}$), four densities, and seven forecast horizons,
a sixteen-statistic topology battery identifies a predictive hierarchy:
initial component count $C(0)$ (mean residual $R^2 = \topCompCountR$),
small-component counts ($\topSmallLeqFourR$), singleton/orthogonal-degree-zero
cells ($\topSingletonR$), and \emph{embedded isolates}---alive cells with
no orthogonal and at least one diagonal live neighbour---as the
mechanistically transparent motif
($R^2 = \topIsoEmbeddedR$; prestate $R^2 = \prestateIsoMean$;
min $\prestateIsoMin$ at $k=200$; shuffle $\approx\prestateIsoShuffle$).

The effect is target-specific: both shuffle increments are near zero
($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$), and the residual slope $\biso(k)$ is negative
in all 112 condition-horizon combinations
($k\in\{1,5,10,25,50,100,200\}$; CV $\lesssim0.17$).
Mechanistically, any orthogonal-degree-zero cell with Moore degree 0 or 1
dies at $t{+}1$ as an exact $S_{23}$ rule consequence; multi-diagonal
isolates more often survive-connected.
Both pathways reduce the component count; a local-window feature captures
this ($R^2 = \localwinR$).
The standardised mechanism transfers across held-out conditions
($R^2_z = \transferFateAll$), and raw amplitude is calibrated from
$(L,\rho)$ (LOO $R^2 = \ampLOOsizerho$).
\end{abstract}

\keywords{cellular automata, coarse-graining, embedded isolated cells,
temporal response, mechanism transfer}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Conway's Game of Life (GoL)~\cite{gardner1970} and HighLife are among
the most studied cellular automata, valued for their deterministic dynamics,
rich emergent structure, and computational tractability.
Earlier work established edge-of-chaos
properties~\cite{langton1990,wolfram1984} and statistical
complexity~\cite{crutchfield1989} of Life-like rules, and identified
the role of coherent structures~\cite{rupe2018} and computational
irreducibility~\cite{israeli2004} in deterministic CAs.
Local causal-state methods~\cite{crutchfield1989,rupe2018} identify
predictive-equivalence partitions from past/future lightcones; our work
instead asks which simple hand-defined structural classes are privileged for
a specific coarse-grained target observable.
The partial predictability of a chosen observable demonstrated here is
consistent with computational irreducibility~\cite{israeli2004}:
we do not claim to predict the full future CA configuration, only one
low-dimensional target statistic.

We identify \emph{local topological fragility}---the initial density of
structurally isolated or weakly-connected live cells---as a target-indexed
property of Life-like CA states that predicts future fine-component loss.
A battery of sixteen topology statistics reveals a predictive hierarchy:
initial component count $C(0)$ is the strongest aggregate predictor;
small-component and singleton-component (orthogonal-degree-zero) counts
are the strongest local-structure predictors; and the count of
\emph{embedded isolated cells} (iso\_count)---alive cells with no
4-connected live neighbour and at least one diagonally adjacent live
neighbour---is the most analytically transparent motif within that family.
Embedded isolates sit at the topological boundary of 4-connectivity:
orthogonally isolated, diagonally tethered, they are the mechanistically
cleanest fragile connectivity nodes in the grid, because their one-step
fate under the $S_{23}$ rule has a simple analytic form.
Earlier observer-scale diagnostics from the same research program motivated
this target-specific analysis; they are documented in the public repository.

The main results, established across GoL and HighLife, two grid sizes
($L\in\{64,128\}$), four density bands ($\rho\in\{0.20,\ldots,0.35\}$),
and seven prediction horizons, are:
(1) a topology-baseline battery of 16 statistics shows that local
fine-topological fragility---led by $C(0)$, small-component counts,
and singleton/orthogonal-degree-zero counts---strongly predicts fine-net
change, with embedded isolates as the most analytically transparent motif;
(2) iso\_count predicts future fine-component loss with mean prestate
$R^2 = \prestateIsoMean$, sustained above $\prestateIsoMin$ at $k=200$;
(3) the effect is target-specific---incremental lift for density, entropy,
and coarse-block targets is near zero, as are both shuffle nulls;
(4) the residual slope $\biso(k)$ is negative in all 112 condition-horizon
combinations, with CV $\lesssim 0.17$;
(5) the mechanism is local component-context loss, not simple cell death,
and transfers across all leave-one-out axes.

Figure~\ref{fig:object_selection} provides the entry point: the structural
definition of an embedded isolate and the target-specific $\Delta R^2$
pattern that singles out the fine-component target.

\section{Definitions and Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}
%------------------------------------------------------------

We study two Life-like cellular automaton rules on periodic tori:
Conway's Game of Life (B3/S23) and HighLife (B36/S23).
Grid sizes $L \in \{64, 128\}$ with toroidal boundary conditions.
The \emph{fine observer} counts 4-connected live-cell components
(BFS on the wrapped grid); the \emph{coarse observer} counts occupied
$8\times 8$ blocks.
An \emph{embedded isolate} is an alive cell for which all four
orthogonally adjacent cells are dead and at least one of the four
diagonally adjacent cells is alive (Fig.~\ref{fig:object_selection}a).
We denote the 4-connected component count at time $t$ as $C(t)$
and define the fine-net change at horizon $k$ as
$\Ck = C(t+k) - C(t)$.

The experiment uses $\mathbf{16}$ conditions:
\begin{equation*}
   \{\mathrm{GoL},\mathrm{HighLife}\}
   \times \{64, 128\}
   \times \{0.20, 0.25, 0.30, 0.35\},
\end{equation*}
giving $2 \text{ rules} \times 2 \text{ sizes} \times 4 \text{ density
levels} = 16$ independent condition--horizon batteries.
Each condition uses 1000 independently drawn random initial states at
the specified density~$\rho$.
We test $k \in \{1, 5, 10, 25, 50, 100, 200\}$, yielding
$7\text{ horizons} \times 16\text{ conditions} = 112$ condition-horizon
slope estimates.

The residual slope $\biso$ is estimated by ordinary least squares (OLS)
with $N_{\rm live}(0)$ (total live-cell count, proportional to $\rho$)
as a nuisance covariate, so that iso\_count is evaluated for its
density-orthogonal contribution.
This and all horizon-response regressions are fitted \emph{condition-wise}
(within each of the 16 batteries independently); the global
$\bar\beta_\iso$ reported in the text is the mean across conditions.
Shuffle-null analyses (iso-count label permutation or target permutation)
are also run within condition.
Mechanism $R^2$ values are 5-fold cross-validated within condition.
Slope confidence intervals use world-resampling bootstrap ($\geq 300$
draws per condition) rather than parametric OLS standard errors.
Each initial grid is drawn independently, making worlds the natural
bootstrap unit.

We use four distinct $R^2$ quantities throughout and distinguish them as
follows.
(a)~\emph{Ordinary $R^2$}: variance explained by an OLS model with no
cross-validation; used for prestate fits.
(b)~\emph{Residual $R^2$}: $R^2$ of iso\_count after the density
covariate has been projected out; this is the within-condition prestate
$R^2$ reported in Section~\ref{sec:selection} and Table~\ref{tab:horizon}.
(c)~\emph{Cross-validated $R^2$} (5-fold, within condition): used for
mechanism features in Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}.
(d)~\emph{$\Delta R^2$}: the difference $R^2(\text{density + predictor})
- R^2(\text{density only})$; used in the target-selection screen
(Section~\ref{sec:selection}) to measure incremental lift over the
density baseline.

All results are scoped to these two Life-like rules on periodic tori
with $L\in\{64,128\}$ and $\rho\in\{0.20,0.25,0.30,0.35\}$.
Low-density dynamics ($\rho\lesssim 0.10$), other Life-like rules, and
non-random initial conditions are outside the present scope.
We do not rerun experiments; all statistics are drawn from the fixed set
of CSVs in \texttt{outputs/}.

\section{Target-Specific Selection and Non-Leaky Prestate}
\label{sec:selection}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Target-specific \texorpdfstring{$\Delta R^2$}{Delta R squared}}

Figure~\ref{fig:object_selection}b shows the selection-principle battery:
for each candidate predictor we measure $\Delta R^2 =
R^2(\text{density + predictor}) - R^2(\text{density only})$ across six
displayed targets, with iso\_count, coarse block-variance, block entropy,
and two shuffle nulls as predictors (see Appendix~\ref{app:residual}
for the residualisation protocol).

iso\_count on the fine-net target yields $\Delta R^2 = \finenetDeltaRtwo$,
a positive incremental lift in both GoL and HighLife screens and in the
global pooled screen.
Both shuffle nulls are near zero ($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$), confirming the
signal is not an artefact of the density-count correlation.
iso\_count adds essentially nothing for density, block-variance,
block-entropy, or coarse-component targets: the uplift is concentrated
on the fine-net target only.
This specificity rules out explanations based purely on total alive-cell
count or generic spatial statistics.

\textit{Two distinct $R^2$ quantities.}
$\Delta R^2 = \finenetDeltaRtwo$ and the prestate $R^2 \approx \prestateIsoMean$
reported in the next subsection answer different questions and should not
be compared directly.
The $\Delta R^2$ screen asks whether iso\_count adds \emph{selective}
information beyond the live-cell-count baseline \emph{across multiple
targets simultaneously}; because the baseline already absorbs most of the
pooled variation, the incremental lift is numerically small by construction.
The prestate $R^2$ evaluates the density-residualised fine-component target
directly within each condition--horizon battery: how much of the
remaining variation does $t=0$ iso\_count explain?
That is the main effect-size estimate---iso\_count accounts for roughly
$\prestateIsoMean$ of residual fine-component-change variation on average
across horizons, falling no lower than $\prestateIsoMin$ at $k=200$.

\subsection{Non-leaky prestate recovery}

A natural concern is whether iso\_count is a proxy for information that
requires knowing the one-step fate of individual cells.
Figure~\ref{fig:prestate} settles this: using \emph{only} iso\_count at
$t=0$---no fate features, no local-window information---the prestate model
achieves mean $R^2 = \prestateIsoMean$ across all horizons
$k \in \{1,\ldots,200\}$ (minimum $R^2 = \prestateIsoMin$;
shuffle $\approx \prestateIsoShuffle$).
Adding the full diagonal-class breakdown gives no substantial gain.
The full multi-horizon iso-count signal is recoverable from the $t{=}0$ count alone.

\section{Temporal Response Curve}
\label{sec:horizon}
%------------------------------------------------------------

For each of the 112 condition-horizon combinations we estimate $\biso(k)$
and the corresponding residual $R^2$.
We also compute an iso-shuffled null at every horizon.

All 112 slopes are negative; all 112 bootstrap slope confidence intervals
are negative ($= \horizonnegfrac$ at every horizon).
The global mean slope moves modestly from $\horizonkone$ at $k=1$
to $\horizonktwohundred$ at $k=200$ (range $\approx 0.09$)
while the CV stays below 0.17 throughout (Table~\ref{tab:horizon}).
Predictive power decays from $R^2 = \horizonRkone$ at $k=1$ to
$R^2 = \horizonRktwohundred$ at $k=200$, yet remains well above the
null at every horizon.

Figure~\ref{fig:response_slope} shows the slope and $R^2$ curves.
The slope plateau from $k=5$ to $k=200$ indicates that the mechanism acts
primarily in the first step: once the initial fragile nodes are resolved
at $t=1$, the remaining dynamics do not strongly modulate the
iso\_count signal.
The CV profile confirms robustness: condition-level spread is small
relative to the global mean, and GoL and HighLife track each other
closely despite different neighbourhood rules.

Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} provides the complete picture: a $16\times 7$
heatmap of all condition-horizon slopes, every cell shaded negative.
There are no outlier conditions or horizons.

\section{Mechanism: Local Component-Context Loss}
\label{sec:mechanism}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Having established \emph{that} iso\_count predicts fine-component change,
we turn to \emph{how} this occurs---what actually happens at $t \to t+1$ when embedded
isolates reduce the future component count?

\textit{One-step transition taxonomy.}
At the one-step level, each embedded isolate can affect local component count
via four transition types:
(i)~\emph{focal death}: the isolate cell dies, removing a local
connectivity node;
(ii)~\emph{survival-connected merger}: the cell survives and fuses with
an adjacent component, absorbing an independent fine component and reducing
the count by one;
(iii)~\emph{birth-mediated bridge}: a newly born neighbour cell connects
the isolate to another component; and
(iv)~\emph{local gain/fragmentation}: births around the isolate create
new independent components, partially offsetting the loss.
Type~(i) is partially analytic: for single-diagonal isolates the focal
cell has exactly one live Moore-neighbour, so it dies under $S_{23}$
regardless of surrounding context (see below).
Types~(ii)--(iv) depend on the surrounding $5\times 5$ neighbourhood
configuration and are measured empirically.

We decompose iso\_count into one-step outcome categories:
death, survival, survival-connected (merges with a larger component),
or triggering births at orthogonal or diagonal sites.
We also compute a \emph{local-window} feature: the total component-count
decrease within a $5\times 5$ window centred on each isolate over one step.
Cross-validated $R^2$ values for these features are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}a.

The local-window feature dominates:
$R^2_{\rm local\_win} = \localwinR$, compared with
$R^2_{\rm iso\_count} = \isocountR$.
The full fate set reaches $R^2_{\rm all\_fates} = \allfatesR$.
Critically, the local-window gain (iso\_local\_window\_gain\_sum) has a
positive slope, partially offsetting the loss: the net mechanistic pathway is
\emph{local component-context loss}, not simple cell death.
Figure~\ref{fig:mechanism}b shows the standardised slopes for each fate
feature, revealing the partial cancellation between loss and gain.

Class-level analysis (Fig.~\ref{fig:classes}) reveals a consistent
physical story.

\paragraph{Exact deaths among orthogonal-degree-zero cells.}
Any alive cell with orthogonal degree 0 has Moore-neighbourhood count
equal to its diagonal-neighbour count.
Under $S_{23}$, survival requires 2 or 3 live Moore-neighbours; therefore:
truly isolated cells (Moore degree 0, no live neighbours at all) die;
single-diagonal embedded isolates (classes 1, 2, 4, 8; Moore degree 1)
die; and four-diagonal isolates (class 15; Moore degree 4) also die.
All three cases are exact consequences of the transition table, independent
of surrounding context; death fractions of $1.0$ for these classes
(Fig.~\ref{fig:classes}) are analytically guaranteed.
Two- and three-diagonal isolates (Moore degrees 2 and 3) survive under
$S_{23}$ and depend on the surrounding $5\times 5$ context for component
merging.
This analytic structure directly explains why the orthogonal-degree-zero
superset outperforms the embedded-isolate subset in raw predictive $R^2$
(Table~\ref{tab:topology_baselines}): it combines both dying subclasses
(Moore degree 0 and 1) under a single count.
Embedded isolates remain the mechanistically preferred motif because the
diagonal-tethering condition gives them a concrete component-context
interpretation absent from truly isolated floating cells.

Two- and three-diagonal embedded isolates more often survive-connected: they merge with a larger component
and reduce the fine component count by absorbing an independent component.
Both pathways lead to the same aggregate outcome---local fine-connectivity
loss---which is why the local-window loss feature ($R^2 = \localwinR$)
dominates over simple death counts ($R^2 = \isocountR$ for iso\_count alone).
The dominant process is the disappearance of fine-net connectivity
in the local neighbourhood, aggregated over many isolate classes and
many conditions.

\section{Robustness: Transfer, Amplitude, and Local Baselines}
\label{sec:transfer}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The mechanism analysis tells us what the carrier is.
Two robustness questions remain: does it generalise to held-out conditions,
and does the scale of the response follow a systematic pattern?

\subsection{Standardised Transfer}
\label{sec:std_transfer}

We $z$-score predictors and targets within each condition, then test
whether the standardised mechanism coefficients transfer to held-out
conditions under four leave-one-out axes (leave-density, leave-size,
leave-rule, leave-condition).
All four transfer axes pass (Fig.~\ref{fig:transfer_amplitude}a).
For the full fate representation, $R^2_z = \transferFateAll$ and
frac $R^2$-positive $= \transferFateAllFrac$ on the leave-condition
axis.
iso\_count alone also transfers ($R^2_z = \isocountR$).
The standardised mechanism direction is condition-invariant: whichever
leave-one-out split is applied, a model trained on the remaining
conditions predicts the held-out condition well.

\subsection{Amplitude Calibration}
\label{sec:amplitude}

Condition amplitudes (raw residual slopes) vary substantially
(amplitude CV $= \ampCV$) but are predictable from grid size and density.
The parsimonious size$+\rho$ model---two predictors, centred $\log_2 L$
and centred $\rho$---achieves LOO $R^2 = \ampLOOsizerho$ on the
16 condition-level amplitudes.
The full model (six predictors: centred $\log_2 L$, centred $\rho$,
rule indicator, and three cross-terms) adds only marginal gain
(LOO $R^2 = \ampLOOfull$).
Size-only gives LOO $R^2 \approx \ampLOOsizeonly$; rule-only gives
$\ampLOOruleonly$, confirming that grid size $L$ is the dominant predictor
and rule identity alone is not.
The two-predictor amplitude model uses 16 condition-level observations and
two predictors; it should be read as a compact within-grid calibration
rather than a universal scaling law.

The raw response thus decomposes as:
\begin{equation}
  \biso(\text{condition})
  \;\approx\;
  A(L,\rho)\;\biso,z\,,
  \label{eq:two_layer}
\end{equation}
where $\biso,z \approx -0.50$ is the transferable standardised slope
and $A(L,\rho)$ is a condition amplitude calibrated from $(L,\rho)$
with LOO $R^2 = \ampLOOsizerho$ for the two-predictor model.
The natural explanation is that $L$ sets the number of components and
hence the scale of fine-component change, while
$\rho$ controls the density of the structural class; together
they set the amplitude scale that multiplies the transferable
standardised direction.

\subsection{Local Topology Baselines}
\label{sec:baselines}

Because the target is a change in 4-connected component count, embedded
isolates might be merely one member of a broader family of local topological
fragility statistics, each predicting $\Ck$ for similar reasons.
We test this directly by running the same density-residualised protocol
(Appendix~\ref{app:residual}) on fifteen alternative $t{=}0$ statistics:
initial component count $C(0)$, small-component counts (sizes $\leq 4$,
$=2,3,4$), singleton components (algebraically identical to orthogonal-degree-zero cells), truly isolated cells (Moore degree 0), Moore-degree counts
($d=1,2$), boundary cells (alive with $\geq 1$ orthogonally dead neighbour),
and coarse block statistics.
Table~\ref{tab:topology_baselines} shows global mean residual $R^2$ per
predictor, averaged over all 112 condition-horizon cells.

The result is structurally informative and transparent.
$C(0)$ is the strongest predictor ($R^2 = \topCompCountR$): more components
at $t{=}0$ means more components ``at risk'' of loss.
Small components of size $\leq 4$ are next ($R^2 = \topSmallLeqFourR$),
followed by singleton components / orthogonal-degree-zero cells
($R^2 = \topSingletonR$).
Embedded isolates rank fourth ($R^2 = \topIsoEmbeddedR$), behind
the orthogonal-degree-zero superset from which they are drawn.
All block-level and Moore-degree-only statistics are substantially weaker.

Embedded isolates are therefore not claimed to be the unique or globally
optimal predictor.
They are a compact, diagonal-tethered subclass of the orthogonal-degree-zero
family, singled out because their one-step fate has a clean analytic form
under $S_{23}$ and because the diagonal-tethering condition provides
a concrete component-context interpretation: a cell touching live structure
diagonally but not orthogonally is a fragile link whose death or merger
directly removes a fine-connectivity node.
The broader local-fragility family---from $C(0)$ to small-component counts
to singleton cells to embedded isolates---forms a coherent predictive
structure that this paper characterises and places on a mechanistic footing.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\textit{Summary.}
Local topological fragility---the initial density of weakly-connected live
cells---predicts future fine-component loss in GoL and HighLife.
The topology-baseline battery (Table~\ref{tab:topology_baselines}) places
this on a tested footing: the predictive hierarchy from $C(0)$ through
small-component counts and singleton/orthogonal-degree-zero cells to
embedded isolates ($R^2 = \topIsoEmbeddedR$) forms a coherent fragility
family, with embedded isolates as the mechanistically transparent motif.
The target-specific prestate signal ($R^2 = \prestateIsoMean$; both shuffle
nulls near zero) arises via a partial analytic mechanistic pathway: any
orthogonal-degree-zero cell with Moore degree 0 or 1 dies exactly under
$S_{23}$ (analytic); multi-diagonal isolates more often survive-connected
and generate local-window loss (empirical).
The standardised response transfers across all leave-one-out axes, with
raw amplitude calibrated from $(L,\rho)$ (LOO $R^2 = \ampLOOsizerho$).

\textit{The two-layer structure.}
The decomposition $\biso(\text{cond}) \approx A(L,\rho)\,\biso,z$ cleanly
separates the transferable standardised mechanism from the condition-specific scale.
This matters for applications: if a practitioner has access to a new
$(L,\rho)$ condition, they can predict the raw slope amplitude with
LOO $R^2 \approx \ampLOOsizerho$ from $(L,\rho)$ alone, and they can
apply the standardised slope direction directly from the training pool.
The two layers are independently portable.

\textit{What is not earned.}
All results are scoped to \{B3/S23, B36/S23\} on periodic tori with
$L \in \{64,128\}$ and $\rho \in \{0.20, 0.25, 0.30, 0.35\}$.
Extension to other Life-like rules, larger grids,
continuous-state CAs, or non-random initial conditions is an open
empirical question.
Closed-form derivation of $\biso$ from microscopic transition rules
is not provided.
Raw amplitude transfer without per-condition calibration is not
supported: amplitude varies with $(L,\rho)$ by a factor of
$\approx \ampCV$ (CV).

\textit{Rule dependence of the embedded-isolate motif.}
The exact one-step deaths established here---Moore-degree-0 and
Moore-degree-1 orthogonal-degree-zero cells dying under $S_{23}$---rely
on the specific survival set $S=\{2,3\}$ shared by GoL and HighLife.
A Life-like rule with a different survival set would alter or eliminate
these exact fates and change the composition of the local-fragility
hierarchy: the embedded-isolate motif is rule-dependent through its
dependence on $S_{23}$.
The broader local-fragility concept---that the initial density of
weakly-connected live cells predicts future fine-component loss---may
generalise to other Life-like rules, but whether $C(0)$, small-component
counts, or embedded isolates retain their relative rankings under
alternative survival sets is an open empirical question and a natural
extension of this work.

\textit{Relation to structural and computational work.}
Component-size and local-connectivity statistics have appeared as descriptors
in CA pattern analyses~\cite{rupe2018}, but their systematic comparison under
a common density-residualised predictive protocol---revealing a coherent
local-fragility hierarchy from $C(0)$ through small-component counts to
embedded isolates---is, to our knowledge, new.
The hierarchy parallels intuitions from percolation theory, where
component-size distributions characterise connectivity near occupation
thresholds, but the focus here is dynamical: which $t{=}0$ local counts
predict future change in fine-scale component number.
Local causal-state methods~\cite{crutchfield1989,rupe2018} infer
predictive-equivalence partitions from joint past/future lightcone statistics;
embedded isolates are a hand-defined structural class within that broader space:
they are not claimed to be a full predictive-equivalence partition,
but they capture one target-specific component of future fine-connectivity
change with a simple geometric definition and without requiring lightcone
data or a learned partition.
The amplitude calibration does not conflict with coarse-graining impossibility
results~\cite{israeli2004}: we predict one low-dimensional target statistic
($\Ck$), not the full future CA configuration.
The partial predictability shown here is consistent with computational
irreducibility; it concerns a specific chosen observable, not global
state reconstruction.
The novelty lies in combining four elements simultaneously: a coherent
local-fragility family that (i) is characterised by a baseline battery
rather than a single hand-picked object, (ii) has a mechanistically
transparent motif (embedded isolates) with exact one-step fate for the
single-diagonal subclass, (iii) produces a standardised mechanism that
transfers across held-out conditions, and (iv) admits amplitude calibration
from $(L,\rho)$---all for future fine-component loss in two well-studied
Life-like rules.

\textit{Local topological fragility as an observer class.}
The topology-baseline battery shows that what matters for predicting
fine-component change is the initial density of locally disconnected or
weakly-connected live cells---a property we call local topological fragility.
$C(0)$, small-component counts, and orthogonal-degree-zero cells all
capture this property in different ways and form a coherent predictive
family.
Embedded isolates are the analytically cleanest member of that family:
their diagonal tethering gives them a component-context interpretation
(they are nodes at risk of losing their local connectivity), and their
one-step fate is partially analytic rather than purely empirical.
The stability of $\biso(k)$ across horizons suggests that the risk resolves
primarily at $t=1$ and that subsequent dynamics do not erase the initial
predictive information---consistent with the prestate non-leakiness result.
iso\_count is therefore a \emph{topological risk indicator} within the
broader local-fragility family, not a simple density proxy and not claimed
to be the uniquely optimal fragility statistic.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Future fine-component change in Conway's Game of Life and HighLife is
controlled by local topological fragility: the initial density of
weakly-connected live cells.
A topology-baseline battery of sixteen statistics identifies a predictive
hierarchy from the initial component count $C(0)$ ($R^2 = \topCompCountR$)
through small-component and singleton/orth-degree-zero counts
($R^2 = \topSmallLeqFourR$, $\topSingletonR$) to embedded isolates
($R^2 = \topIsoEmbeddedR$), the most analytically transparent motif.
The $t=0$ embedded-isolate count alone explains a substantial fraction of
residual fine-component change across all tested horizons
(mean prestate $R^2 = \prestateIsoMean$; minimum $R^2 = \prestateIsoMin$
at $k=200$; shuffle $\approx \prestateIsoShuffle$).
The residual slope $\biso(k)$ is negative in all 112 condition-horizon
combinations (7 horizons $\times$ 16 conditions;
$2 \text{ rules} \times 2 \text{ sizes} \times 4 \text{ density levels}$),
an empirical regularity that holds without exception across the scoped battery.
Both iso-shuffle and target-shuffle incremental nulls are near zero
($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$), ruling out density artefacts.
The one-step mechanistic pathway is local component-context loss, not simple cell
death: any orthogonal-degree-zero cell with Moore degree 0 or 1 dies exactly
under the shared $S_{23}$ rule; two- and three-diagonal isolates often
survive-connected.
This standardised mechanism transfers across all leave-one-out axes
and the raw amplitude is predictable from $(L,\rho)$
(LOO $R^2 = \ampLOOsizerho$, rising to $\ampLOOfull$ in the full
six-predictor model).

\section{Regression Details and Null Definitions}
\label{app:residual}

\subsection*{Residualisation protocol}

For each prediction world, the density nuisance covariate is
$N_{\rm live}(0)$, the total live-cell count at $t=0$
(proportional to $\rho$ up to Bernoulli sampling variance).
All regression models include $N_{\rm live}(0)$ as a linear covariate
before adding iso\_count or any fate feature.
$\Delta R^2 = R^2(\text{density + predictor}) - R^2(\text{density only})$.
The residual slope $\biso$ is the OLS coefficient on iso\_count in this
density-residualised fit.

\subsection*{Null definitions}

Two nulls are used:
(i)~\emph{iso-shuffle null}: iso\_count labels are permuted uniformly at
random within each condition; $\Delta R^2$ and slope are recomputed.
(ii)~\emph{target-shuffle null}: the target $\Ck$ is permuted; $\Delta R^2$
is recomputed with the original iso\_count.
Both converge near zero ($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$), confirming the signal
depends on the joint alignment of iso\_count with $\Ck$ and is not
a density or permutation artefact.

\section{Transition-Class Coding}
\label{app:classes}

Diagonal-adjacency class is an integer in $[1, 15]$ indexing the bit
pattern of the four diagonal neighbours of an embedded isolate
(NW, NE, SW, SE), with class $c$ equal to the binary value
$\textit{NW}\cdot 1 + \textit{NE}\cdot 2 + \textit{SW}\cdot 4 +
\textit{SE}\cdot 8$.
Class 15 (all four diagonal neighbours alive) is rare in this battery
($p \approx 0.009$, $n = 80{,}015$ instances) but contributes a negative
local response ($\bar\Delta \approx -2.52$, death fraction $= 1.0$)
comparable to other multi-diagonal classes; it is not excluded from
the analysis.
Classes 1, 2, 4, 8 are the \emph{single-diagonal} classes;
classes 3, 5, 6, 9, 10, 12 are the \emph{two-diagonal} classes;
classes 7, 11, 13, 14 are the \emph{three-diagonal} classes;
class 15 is the \emph{four-diagonal} class.
