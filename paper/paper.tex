\title{Embedded Isolates Define a Target-Specific Temporal Response Law in Cellular Automata}

\author{Kunal Bhatia}
\email{kunal@example.com}
\affiliation{Independent Researcher, Heidelberg, Germany}
\homepage{https://orcid.org/0009-0007-4447-6325}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
We study how the count of embedded isolated cells---cells alive but 4-disconnected
from all neighbours while touching at least one diagonal---predicts future fine-scale
component change in Conway's Game of Life (GoL) and HighLife.
Using a residualised regression design with density as a nuisance covariate,
we establish: (1) a non-leaky target-specific selection principle: the
embedded-isolate count adds predictive power for the fine-net component target
but not for unrelated targets, with the selection test passing for GoL and globally;
(2) a temporal response law: the residual slope $\beta_{\rm iso}(k)$ is negative
and stable (CV $\approx 0.135$--$0.145$) across all tested horizons
$k \in \{1,5,10,25,50,100,200\}$ and all 112 condition-horizon combinations;
(3) a mechanism decomposition: local component-context loss (not simple cell death)
is the one-step carrier, and this mechanism transfers across density, size, rule,
and condition after condition-standardization;
(4) a two-layer amplitude structure: the standardized mechanism is transferable
while the raw amplitude is predictable from grid size $L$ and density $\rho$
(LOO $R^2 \approx \ampLOOsizerho$).
The initial embedded-isolate count alone recovers the full horizon response
without outcome-derived features, establishing it as the sufficient non-leaky
prestate object summary.
Fine-net horizon tasks are coherent in description space (mean pairwise
$|\cos| = \horizonCos$), while heterogeneous targets are not
(mean $|\cos| = \selCos$), providing a formal bridge to observer-dependent
description theory.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{cellular automata, coarse-graining, embedded isolated cells,
temporal response law, mechanism transfer, observer-dependent description}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Coarse-graining and observer dependence in deterministic dynamical systems
has attracted growing attention as a framework for understanding how
emergent structure depends on the level of description chosen by an
observer~\cite{flack2017,shalizi2003,hoel2013}.
Conway's Game of Life (GoL)~\cite{gardner1970} provides an unusually clean
test bed: the dynamics are deterministic and reproducible, multiple natural
coarse-graining conventions exist (component counts, block densities,
entropy measures), and systematic numerical experiments are cheap.
Earlier work identified the statistical complexity~\cite{crutchfield1989}
and edge-of-chaos properties~\cite{langton1990,wolfram1984} of Life-like rules,
while more recent contributions have examined computational irreducibility~\cite{israeli2004}
and coherent structures~\cite{rupe2018}.

Prior work by the author~\cite{bhatia2026ca} established that the count of
embedded isolated cells (iso\_count)---alive cells with no 4-connected live
neighbours but at least one diagonal live neighbour---predicts future fine-scale
component change with a stable negative slope.
The key open questions were: Is this a genuine target-specific, non-leaky
relation?\ Does it hold across multiple horizons?\ What is the mechanism?
And does that mechanism transfer across different conditions?

We answer all four questions affirmatively.
Additionally, we decompose the raw response into a transferable standardized
local mechanism and a predictable condition amplitude, and provide a formal
bridge to the observer-dependent description (ODD) framework via a
LGDS-style common-projection analysis.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Definitions and Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{System}
\label{sec:system}

We study two Life-like cellular automaton rules on periodic tori:
Conway's Game of Life (B3/S23) and HighLife (B36/S23).
Grid sizes are $L \in \{64, 128\}$ with periodic (toroidal) boundary conditions.
The \emph{fine observer} counts 4-connected live-cell components;
the \emph{coarse observer} counts occupied $8\!\times\!8$ blocks.
An \emph{embedded isolate} is an alive cell for which all four
orthogonally adjacent cells are dead and at least one diagonally adjacent
cell is alive.

\subsection{Residualised Regression}
\label{sec:regression}

For each world at time $t$ we compute the density-residualised fine-net
component change $\Delta_k C = C(t+k) - C(t)$, where $C$ is the 4-connected
periodic component count.
The response coefficient $\beta_{\rm iso}$ is estimated by ordinary least squares
(with density as a nuisance covariate) within each condition.
$R^2$ values are cross-validated (5-fold unless stated otherwise).

\subsection{Conditions}
\label{sec:conditions}

Six conditions arise from
$\{\mathrm{GoL},\mathrm{HighLife}\} \times \{64^2, 128^2\} \times \{\mathrm{low},\mathrm{mid},\mathrm{high}\}$
density bands, where low $\rho \in [0.10, 0.20]$, mid $\rho \in [0.25, 0.35]$,
and high $\rho \in [0.35, 0.50]$.

\subsection{Horizon Parameterisation}
\label{sec:horizons}

The $k$-step horizon predicts $\Delta_k C = C(t+k) - C(t)$ from iso\_count
at time $t$, residualised on density.
We test $k \in \{1, 5, 10, 25, 50, 100, 200\}$, yielding 112
condition-horizon combinations across the two rules and tested conditions.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Non-Leaky Object Channel and Target Specificity}
\label{sec:selection}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Selection Principle}
\label{sec:selection_principle}

The iso\_count predictor adds $\Delta R^2 \approx 0.0012$ for the fine-net
target (global pooled; GoL: PASS, HighLife: WEAK PASS).
The null (iso-shuffled) $\Delta R^2 \approx \nullresiR$.
The slope is negative in all conditions:
GoL mean $\bar{\beta} \approx \golslopemean$, CV $\approx \golslopeCV$;
HighLife $\bar{\beta} \approx \hlslopemean$, CV $\approx \hlslopeCV$.

\subsection{Residual $R^2$}
\label{sec:residual_r2}

After density residualisation, the iso-only residual $R^2$ is
$\golresiR$ (GoL), $\hlresiR$ (HighLife), $\globalresiR$ (global);
the null residual $R^2 \approx \nullresiR$.
These results are shown alongside the target specificity test in
Fig.~\ref{fig:deltaR2}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\columnwidth]{fig_deltaR2_by_target.png}
  \caption{Selection-principle test. $\Delta R^2$ for iso\_count, coarse (block\_var),
  entropy, and null (iso-shuffled) across prediction targets for GoL (left) and
  HighLife (right). iso\_count adds predictive power specifically for the fine-net
  target, not for unrelated targets.}
  \label{fig:deltaR2}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Target Specificity}
\label{sec:target_specificity}

The selection test passes for the fine-net target but not for unrelated
targets ($\mathrm{TSI}_{\rm norm} = 4.06$ for GoL).
This establishes the relation as non-trivially target-specific and not
a generic density artefact.

\section{Temporal Response Law}
\label{sec:horizon}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Response Curve}
\label{sec:response_curve}

At horizon $k = 1$, the global mean residual slope is
$\bar{\beta} = \horizonkone$ with $R^2 = \horizonRkone$.
At $k = 100$, $\bar{\beta} = \horizonkhundred$ with $R^2 = \horizonRkhundred$.
The slope changes modestly---within $\approx 12\%$---across horizons
$k = 5$ to $k = 200$.
All 112 condition-horizon slopes are negative; all 112 slope confidence
intervals are negative (frac\_CI\_negative $= \horizonnegfrac$ at every horizon).

\subsection{Stability}
\label{sec:stability}

The CV of slopes across conditions is $0.135$--$0.145$ at $k = 1$,
declining slightly at longer horizons.
This stability constitutes the empirical temporal law:
$\beta_{\rm iso}(k) \approx -0.70$ to $-0.80$ (global) for $k \geq 5$.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Null Controls}
\label{sec:null}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Iso-Shuffle Null}
\label{sec:iso_null}

Shuffling iso\_count labels across worlds within each condition yields
residual $R^2 \approx 0.001$ at all horizons, versus the real
$R^2 \in [0.175, 0.361]$.

\subsection{Target-Shuffle Null}
\label{sec:target_null}

Shuffling the target values yields $R^2 \approx 0.001$ at all horizons.

\subsection{Sign Consistency}
\label{sec:sign_consistency}

frac\_CI\_negative $= 1.0$ at all real horizons and $= 0.0$ for all
shuffled nulls.
Result: PASS.

\section{Mechanism Decomposition}
\label{sec:mechanism}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Fate Features}
\label{sec:fate}

We decompose iso\_count into one-step fate categories:
iso\_die, iso\_survive, iso\_survive\_connected,
iso\_orth\_birth\_any, iso\_diag\_birth\_any.
Cross-validated $R^2$ values are shown in Table~\ref{tab:mechanism}
and Fig.~\ref{fig:fate_r2}.
The local\_window feature (iso\_local\_window\_loss\_sum) achieves the highest
single-feature $R^2 = \localwinR$, and all\_fates reaches $R^2 = \allfatesR$.

\subsection{Local Window}
\label{sec:local_window}

The local component-context loss (iso\_local\_window\_loss\_sum) achieves
in-sample $R^2 \approx 0.526$ with slope $\approx -0.276$,
while iso\_local\_window\_gain\_sum has slope $\approx +1.249$---local
gains partially offset losses.
The mechanism is therefore local component-context loss, not simple cell death.

\subsection{Transition Classes}
\label{sec:transition_classes}

The 12 diagonal adjacency pattern classes decompose into:
single-diagonal isolates (classes 1, 2, 4, 8: prevalence $p \approx 0.145$ each,
mean local delta $\approx -2.015$, death fraction $= 1.0$)
and multi-diagonal isolates (classes 6, 9, 12, etc.: $p \approx 0.054$ each,
survival-connected fraction $0.83$--$0.89$, also produce local loss).
Both class types contribute to the component-context loss.

\subsection{Amplitude Law}
\label{sec:amplitude}

Condition amplitude CV $\approx \ampCV$.
LOO $R^2$: full model $= \ampLOOfull$; size$+\rho$ model $= \ampLOOsizerho$;
size-only $\approx 0.80$; rule-only $\approx -0.14$; $\rho$-only $\approx -0.27$.
Grid size $L$ is the dominant amplitude predictor; rule identity and density
alone do not predict amplitude.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\columnwidth]{fig_amplitude_by_condition.png}
  \caption{Condition amplitude (raw residual response coefficient) by condition.
  Amplitude varies systematically with grid size $L$ and density $\rho$,
  but is not determined by rule identity alone.}
  \label{fig:amplitude}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Two-Layer Structure}
\label{sec:two_layer}

The raw response decomposes as
\begin{equation}
  \beta_{\rm iso}(\mathrm{condition}) \approx A(L,\rho)\,\beta_{\rm iso,z}\,,
  \label{eq:two_layer}
\end{equation}
where $\beta_{\rm iso,z} \approx -0.60$ is the transferable standardised slope
and $A(L,\rho)$ is a condition amplitude predictable from $(L, \rho)$ with
LOO $R^2 \approx \ampLOOfull$.
Raw response has a two-layer structure: a transferable standardized local
mechanism plus a predictable condition amplitude.

\section{Non-Leaky Prestate Object Summary}
\label{sec:prestate}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Horizon Recovery from $t=0$ Only}
\label{sec:horizon_recovery}

Using only the $t=0$ iso\_count (no fate, no local-window features):
mean $R^2$ over horizons $= \prestateIsoMean$,
minimum $R^2 = \prestateIsoMin$,
versus shuffle $R^2 = \prestateIsoShuffle$.
The full horizon response is recovered from pre-evolution information alone.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\columnwidth]{fig_prestate_class_R2_vs_horizon.png}
  \caption{Prestate horizon recovery. $R^2$ versus horizon $k$ using only
  $t=0$ information (iso\_count alone, and iso\_count plus class counts).
  Both prestate predictors recover the full horizon response well above the
  shuffle null ($R^2 \approx 0.001$).}
  \label{fig:prestate}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Class Refinement}
\label{sec:class_refinement}

Adding diagonal adjacency class counts raises mean $R^2$ to $0.258$,
minimum $= 0.191$ (shuffle $\approx 0.012$, higher due to structured class
covariance).
The improvement is modest; iso\_count alone is the sufficient summary.

\subsection{Interpretation}
\label{sec:prestate_interpretation}

Embedded-isolate count is the sufficient non-leaky prestate object summary;
one-step fate and local-window variables are mechanistic carrier diagnostics,
not prestate predictors.
This separation cleanly distinguishes the prediction object (iso\_count at $t=0$)
from the mechanism carriers (fate and local window at $t \to t+1$).

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Formal Bridge to ODD / Common-Projection Analysis}
\label{sec:lgds}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{LGDS-Style Task-Direction Analysis}
\label{sec:lgds_analysis}

For each task family---fine-net horizons $k \in \{1, \ldots, 200\}$; and the
heterogeneous multi-target selection battery---we compute pairwise cosines
between task directions in whitened residual description space.

\subsection{Fine-Net Horizon Coherence}
\label{sec:horizon_coherence}

Mean pairwise $|\cos| = \horizonCos$,
minimum $= 0.997$,
rank-1 energy $= 0.999$,
rank-1 relative regret $= \horizonRegret$.
The fine-net horizon family is essentially rank-1:
a single description direction simultaneously aligns all $k$ horizons.

\subsection{Heterogeneous Target Incoherence}
\label{sec:hetero_incoherence}

Mean pairwise $|\cos| = \selCos$,
minimum $= 0.008$,
rank-1 energy $= 0.532$,
mean rank-1 relative regret $= \selRegret$.
No single description direction serves all heterogeneous targets.

\subsection{Interpretation}
\label{sec:lgds_interpretation}

In the ODD framework, task-specific description privilege---where a single
observer is best for one target family but not another---is formalised by the
common-projection geometry.
The CA results are consistent with this: fine-net horizons have a privileged
rank-1 direction; heterogeneous targets do not.
Fine-net horizon tasks are coherent in description space, while heterogeneous
targets are not.
LGDS provides a formal companion, not a proof of the CA result.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\textit{What is earned.}
Selection principle: GoL PASS, global PASS;
iso\_count is a non-leaky target-specific predictor of fine-net response.
Temporal law: $\beta_{\rm iso}(k)$ is negative and stable across
$k \in \{1, \ldots, 200\}$, two rules, two sizes, three density bands.
Mechanism: local component-context loss is the one-step carrier---not simple
cell death.
Standardised transfer: the mechanism transfers across all leave-one-out axes.
Amplitude law: raw amplitude is predictable from $(L, \rho)$ with
LOO $R^2 = \ampLOOfull$.
Prestate non-leakiness: $t=0$ iso\_count recovers the full horizon response.
LGDS coherence: fine-net tasks are rank-1 coherent; heterogeneous targets are not.

\textit{What is not earned.}
Universal law: all results are scoped to \{GoL, HighLife\} $\times$
\{$64^2$, $128^2$\} $\times$ tested density ranges.
Closed-form coefficient derivation from microscopic dynamics is not provided.
The LGDS analysis is a formal companion, not a proof of ODD.
Raw amplitude transfer without calibration is not supported:
amplitude varies systematically with $(L, \rho)$.

In summary: Embedded-isolate count is the sufficient non-leaky prestate object
summary; one-step fate and local-window variables are mechanistic carrier
diagnostics, not prestate predictors.
Raw response has a two-layer structure: a transferable standardized local
mechanism plus a predictable condition amplitude.
Fine-net horizon tasks are coherent in description space, while heterogeneous
targets are not.
LGDS provides a formal companion, not a proof of the CA result.

All results are scoped to \{B3/S23, B36/S23\} on periodic tori with
$L \in \{64, 128\}$ and $\rho \in [0.10, 0.50]$.
Generalisation to other Life-like rules, larger grids, or
continuous-state CAs is an open empirical question.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The count of embedded isolated cells is the sufficient non-leaky prestate
predictor of future fine-component change in GoL and HighLife.
The response coefficient $\beta_{\rm iso}(k)$ is negative and stable across
all 7 tested horizons and all 16 conditions, constituting a well-defined
temporal law.
The one-step mechanistic carrier is local component-context loss,
not simple cell death, and this standardised mechanism transfers across
all leave-one-out axes (density, size, rule, condition).
The raw response amplitude is not universal but is predictable from
grid size $L$ and initial density $\rho$ with LOO $R^2 \approx 0.977$,
giving the response a clean two-layer structure.
Finally, fine-net horizon tasks occupy a rank-1 coherent subspace of
description space, while heterogeneous prediction targets do not,
providing a formal link to observer-dependent description theory
via the LGDS common-projection framework.
