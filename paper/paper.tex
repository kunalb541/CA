\title{Embedded Isolates Define a Target-Specific Temporal Response Law
       in Cellular Automata}

\author{Kunal Bhatia}
\affiliation{Independent Researcher, Heidelberg, Germany}
\homepage{https://orcid.org/0009-0007-4447-6325}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
Life-like cellular automata contain local structures whose predictive
importance depends on which observable is used to describe the system.
We identify one such structure: an \emph{embedded isolate}, an alive cell
with no orthogonal live neighbour but at least one diagonal live neighbour.
Across Conway's Game of Life and HighLife, two grid sizes, four initial
densities, and seven prediction horizons, the initial count of embedded
isolates predicts future loss of fine-scale connected components.
The effect is target-specific: iso\_count adds $\Delta R^2 = \finenetDeltaRtwo$
for fine-component change (GoL: \golSelVerdict; HighLife: WEAK PASS)
while iso-shuffle and target-shuffle nulls are near zero ($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$),
ruling out density artefacts.
The residual slope $\biso(k)$ is negative in all 112 condition-horizon
combinations ($k\in\{1,5,10,25,50,100,200\}$, 16 conditions; CV $\lesssim 0.17$)
and is fully recovered from $t=0$ iso\_count alone (mean prestate $R^2 =
\prestateIsoMean$; shuffle $\approx \prestateIsoShuffle$).

Mechanistically, the signal is carried by local component-context loss rather
than simple cell death.
Single-diagonal isolates die with near certainty and remove a local
component-boundary node; multi-diagonal isolates more often survive by merging
into a larger component.
Both pathways reduce fine connected-component count, and the local-window
loss feature captures this with $R^2 = \localwinR$.
After condition standardisation, the mechanism transfers across held-out rule,
size, density, and condition ($R^2_z = \transferFateAll$),
while the raw amplitude is predictable from grid size and initial density
(LOO $R^2 = \ampLOOfull$).

These results give a compact example of target-indexed description:
the same automaton state admits many possible summaries, but embedded
isolates are privileged specifically for future fine-component change.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{cellular automata, coarse-graining, embedded isolated cells,
temporal response law, mechanism transfer, observer-dependent description}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Conway's Game of Life (GoL)~\cite{gardner1970} and HighLife are among
the most studied cellular automata, valued for their deterministic dynamics,
rich emergent structure, and computational tractability.
Earlier work established edge-of-chaos
properties~\cite{langton1990,wolfram1984} and statistical
complexity~\cite{crutchfield1989} of Life-like rules, and identified
the role of coherent structures~\cite{rupe2018} and computational
irreducibility~\cite{israeli2004} in deterministic CAs.
Less attention has been paid to the question of which local prestate
features predict a specific target observable at multiple horizons.

We identify one such feature: the count of \emph{embedded isolated cells}
(iso\_count), alive cells with no 4-connected live neighbour and at least
one diagonally adjacent live neighbour.
Such cells sit at the topological boundary of 4-connectivity: orthogonally
isolated, diagonally tethered, they are the most fragile local connectivity
nodes in the grid.
When they die or merge, the fine-component count decreases by one.
The iso\_count statistic measures how many such fragile nodes are present
at $t=0$ and, as this paper shows, this is sufficient to predict the
aggregate fine-net change at every tested horizon.

The main results, established across GoL and HighLife, two grid sizes, four
density bands, and seven prediction horizons, are:
(1) iso\_count selects the fine-net target specifically---not density, entropy,
or coarse-block targets---with both shuffle nulls near zero;
(2) the response coefficient $\biso(k)$ is negative and stable from $k=1$
to $k=200$ in all 112 condition-horizon combinations;
(3) the one-step mechanism is local component-context loss, not simple cell
death, and transfers across all leave-one-out axes;
(4) the full multi-horizon response is encoded in the $t=0$ count---no fate
information leaks in from later steps.

Figure~\ref{fig:object_selection} provides the entry point: the structural
definition of an embedded isolate and the target-specific $\Delta R^2$
pattern that singles it out.

\section{Definitions and Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}
%------------------------------------------------------------

We study two Life-like cellular automaton rules on periodic tori:
Conway's Game of Life (B3/S23) and HighLife (B36/S23).
Grid sizes $L \in \{64, 128\}$ with toroidal boundary conditions.
The \emph{fine observer} counts 4-connected live-cell components
(BFS on the wrapped grid); the \emph{coarse observer} counts occupied
$8\times 8$ blocks.
An \emph{embedded isolate} is an alive cell for which all four
orthogonally adjacent cells are dead and at least one of the four
diagonally adjacent cells is alive (Fig.~\ref{fig:object_selection}a).
We denote the 4-connected component count at time $t$ as $C(t)$
and define the fine-net change at horizon $k$ as
$\Ck = C(t+k) - C(t)$.

The experiment uses $\mathbf{16}$ conditions:
\begin{equation*}
   \{\mathrm{GoL},\mathrm{HighLife}\}
   \times \{64, 128\}
   \times \{0.20, 0.25, 0.30, 0.35\},
\end{equation*}
giving $2 \text{ rules} \times 2 \text{ sizes} \times 4 \text{ density
levels} = 16$ independent condition--horizon batteries.
Each condition uses 1000 independently drawn random initial states at
the specified density~$\rho$.
We test $k \in \{1, 5, 10, 25, 50, 100, 200\}$, yielding
$7\text{ horizons} \times 16\text{ conditions} = 112$ condition-horizon
slope estimates.

The residual slope $\biso$ is estimated by ordinary least squares with
$N_{\rm live}(0)$ (live-cell count, proportional to density) as a
nuisance covariate, so that iso\_count is evaluated for its
density-orthogonal contribution.
$R^2$ values for mechanism features are cross-validated (5-fold).
We do not rerun experiments; all statistics are drawn from the fixed set
of CSVs in \texttt{outputs/}.

\section{Target-Specific Selection and Non-Leaky Prestate}
\label{sec:selection}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Target-specific $\Delta R^2$}

Figure~\ref{fig:object_selection}b shows the selection-principle battery:
for each candidate predictor we measure $\Delta R^2 =
R^2(\text{density + predictor}) - R^2(\text{density only})$ on six
displayed targets (iso\_count, coarse block-variance, block entropy,
iso-shuffle null, and target-shuffle null; see Appendix~\ref{app:residual}
for the residualisation protocol).

iso\_count on the fine-net target yields $\Delta R^2 = \finenetDeltaRtwo$
(GoL: \golSelVerdict; HighLife: WEAK PASS; global: PASS).
Both shuffle nulls are near zero ($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$), confirming the
signal is not an artefact of the density-count correlation.
iso\_count adds essentially nothing for density, block-variance,
block-entropy, or coarse-component targets: the uplift is concentrated
on the fine-net target only.
This specificity rules out explanations based purely on total alive-cell
count or generic spatial statistics.

\subsection{Non-leaky prestate recovery}

A natural concern is whether iso\_count is a proxy for information that
requires knowing the one-step fate of individual cells.
Figure~\ref{fig:prestate} settles this: using \emph{only} iso\_count at
$t=0$---no fate features, no local-window information---the prestate model
achieves mean $R^2 = \prestateIsoMean$ across all horizons
$k \in \{1,\ldots,200\}$ (minimum $R^2 = \prestateIsoMin$;
shuffle $\approx \prestateIsoShuffle$).
Adding the full diagonal-class breakdown gives no substantial gain.
The full multi-horizon response is encoded in the $t=0$ count alone.

\section{Temporal Response Law}
\label{sec:horizon}
%------------------------------------------------------------

For each of the 112 condition-horizon combinations we estimate $\biso(k)$
and the corresponding residual $R^2$.
We also compute an iso-shuffled null at every horizon.

All 112 slopes are negative; all 112 slope confidence intervals are
negative (frac\_CI\_negative $= \horizonnegfrac$ at every horizon).
The global mean slope moves modestly from $\horizonkone$ at $k=1$
to $\horizonktwohundred$ at $k=200$---a range of $\approx 0.09$---while
the CV stays below 0.17 throughout (Table~\ref{tab:horizon}).
Predictive power decays from $R^2 = \horizonRkone$ at $k=1$ to
$R^2 = \horizonRktwohundred$ at $k=200$, yet remains well above the
null at every horizon.

Figure~\ref{fig:response_slope} shows the slope and $R^2$ curves.
The slope plateau from $k=5$ to $k=200$ indicates that the mechanism acts
primarily in the first step: once the initial fragile nodes are resolved
at $t=1$, the remaining dynamics do not strongly modulate the
iso\_count signal.
The CV profile confirms robustness: condition-level spread is small
relative to the global mean, and GoL and HighLife track each other
closely despite different neighbourhood rules.

Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} provides the complete picture: a $16\times 7$
heatmap of all condition-horizon slopes, every cell shaded negative.
There are no outlier conditions or horizons.

\section{Mechanism: Local Component-Context Loss}
\label{sec:mechanism}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Having established \emph{that} iso\_count predicts fine-component change,
we turn to \emph{how} this occurs---what actually happens at $t \to t+1$ when embedded
isolates reduce the future component count?

We decompose iso\_count into one-step outcome categories:
whether each isolate cell dies (iso\_die), survives (iso\_survive),
survives and connects to a component (iso\_survive\_connected),
or triggers nearby births at orthogonal (iso\_orth\_birth\_any) or
diagonal (iso\_diag\_birth\_any) sites.
We also compute a \emph{local-window} feature:
iso\_local\_window\_loss\_sum, the total component-count decrease
within a $5\times 5$ window centred on each isolate over one step.
Cross-validated $R^2$ values for these features are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}a.

The local-window feature dominates:
$R^2_{\rm local\_win} = \localwinR$, compared with
$R^2_{\rm iso\_count} = \isocountR$.
The full fate set reaches $R^2_{\rm all\_fates} = \allfatesR$.
Critically, the local-window gain (iso\_local\_window\_gain\_sum) has a
positive slope, partially offsetting the loss: the net mechanism is
\emph{local component-context loss}, not simple cell death.
Figure~\ref{fig:mechanism}b shows the standardised slopes for each fate
feature, revealing the partial cancellation between loss and gain.

Class-level analysis (Fig.~\ref{fig:classes}) reveals a consistent
physical story.
\emph{Single-diagonal isolates} (diagonal-adjacency classes 1, 2, 4, 8;
death fraction $= 1.0$ in GoL) die with near certainty at $t{+}1$,
removing a local component-boundary node and reducing $C(t{+}1)$.
\emph{Multi-diagonal isolates} (classes with 2--4 diagonal neighbours)
more often survive, but survive-connected: they merge with a larger
component and again reduce the fine component count by absorbing
an independent component.
Both pathways lead to the same aggregate outcome---local fine-connectivity
loss---which is why the local-window loss feature ($R^2 = \localwinR$)
dominates over simple death counts ($R^2 = \isocountR$ for iso\_count alone).
The dominant process is the disappearance of fine-net connectivity
in the local neighbourhood, aggregated over many isolate classes and
many conditions.

\section{Robustness: Transfer, Amplitude, and Nulls}
\label{sec:transfer}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The mechanism analysis tells us what the carrier is.
Two robustness questions remain: does it generalise to held-out conditions,
and does the scale of the response follow a systematic pattern?

\subsection{Standardised Transfer}
\label{sec:std_transfer}

We $z$-score predictors and targets within each condition, then test
whether the standardised mechanism coefficients transfer to held-out
conditions under four leave-one-out axes (leave-density, leave-size,
leave-rule, leave-condition).
All four transfer axes pass (Fig.~\ref{fig:transfer_amplitude}a).
For the full fate representation, $R^2_z = \transferFateAll$ and
frac $R^2$-positive $= \transferFateAllFrac$ on the leave-condition
axis.
iso\_count alone also transfers ($R^2_z = \isocountR$).
The standardised mechanism direction is condition-invariant: whichever
leave-one-out split is applied, a model trained on the remaining
conditions predicts the held-out condition well.

\subsection{Amplitude Law}
\label{sec:amplitude}

Condition amplitudes (raw residual slopes) vary substantially
(amplitude CV $= \ampCV$) but are predictable from $(L, \rho)$.
LOO $R^2$: full model $= \ampLOOfull$; size$+\rho$ model
$= \ampLOOsizerho$; size-only $\approx \ampLOOsizeonly$;
rule-only $= \ampLOOruleonly$.
Grid size $L$ is the dominant predictor of amplitude;
rule identity and density alone do not determine it.

The raw response thus decomposes as:
\begin{equation}
  \biso(\text{condition})
  \;\approx\;
  A(L,\rho)\;\biso,z\,,
  \label{eq:two_layer}
\end{equation}
where $\biso,z \approx -0.50$ is the transferable standardised slope
and $A(L,\rho)$ is a condition amplitude predictable with
LOO $R^2 \approx \ampLOOfull$.
The natural explanation is that $L$ sets the number of components and
hence the denominator of the relative change $\Ck/C(0)$, while
$\rho$ controls the density of the specific structural class; together
they set the amplitude scale that multiplies the universal
standardised direction.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\textit{Summary.}
iso\_count is a non-leaky, target-specific predictor of fine-net component
change ($\Delta R^2 = \finenetDeltaRtwo$, both shuffle nulls near zero;
full response encoded in $t=0$ alone, prestate $R^2 = \prestateIsoMean$).
$\biso(k)$ is negative and stable across $k \in \{1,\ldots,200\}$,
both rules, both grid sizes, and all four density levels without exception.
The one-step mechanism is local component-context loss ($R^2 = \localwinR$),
not simple cell death, and this standardised direction transfers across all
leave-one-out axes with LOO $R^2 = \ampLOOfull$ for the amplitude.

\textit{Geometric interpretation.}
A compact way to see why a single statistic serves all fine-net horizons
is to measure the alignment of task-direction vectors in feature space~\cite{hoel2013}.
For each task we fit a residual model and normalise the coefficient vector;
pairwise absolute cosines $|\cos\theta_{ij}|$ measure task alignment.
Fine-net horizon tasks are near-collinear
($|\cos| = \horizonCos$, rank-1 energy $= \horizonRankone$):
a single feature direction aligns all $k$-step predictions simultaneously.
Heterogeneous targets are not ($|\cos| = \selCos$),
and no single predictor adds $\Delta R^2 > 0.001$ for all of them.
Figure~\ref{fig:coherence} shows this contrast.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.70\columnwidth]{fig8_coherence}
  \caption{
    \textbf{Task-direction coherence.}
    Mean pairwise $|\cos\theta|$ (solid) and rank-1 energy (hatched)
    for fine-net horizon tasks (blue) and heterogeneous targets (orange).
    Fine-net horizons are near-collinear
    ($|\cos| = \horizonCos$, rank-1 $= \horizonRankone$);
    heterogeneous targets are dispersed ($|\cos| = \selCos$).
  }
  \label{fig:coherence}
\end{figure}

\textit{The two-layer structure.}
The decomposition $\biso(\text{cond}) \approx A(L,\rho)\,\biso,z$ cleanly
separates the universal mechanism from the condition-specific scale.
This matters for applications: if a practitioner has access to a new
$(L,\rho)$ condition, they can predict the raw slope amplitude with
LOO $R^2 \approx \ampLOOfull$ from $(L,\rho)$ alone, and they can
apply the standardised slope direction directly from the training pool.
The two layers are independently portable.

\textit{What is not earned.}
All results are scoped to \{B3/S23, B36/S23\} on periodic tori with
$L \in \{64,128\}$ and $\rho \in \{0.20, 0.25, 0.30, 0.35\}$.
Extension to other Life-like rules, larger grids,
continuous-state CAs, or non-random initial conditions is an open
empirical question.
Closed-form derivation of $\biso$ from microscopic transition rules
is not provided.
Raw amplitude transfer without per-condition calibration is not
supported: amplitude varies with $(L,\rho)$ by a factor of
$\approx \ampCV$ (CV).

\textit{Embedded isolates as objects.}
Embedded isolated cells are not just a convenient statistic: they form
a well-defined structural class at the topological boundary of
connectivity.
Their prevalence determines how much the fine-component count is
``at risk'' at the next step.
The stability of $\biso(k)$ across horizons suggests that the risk
resolves primarily at $t=1$ and that subsequent dynamics do not erase
the initial predictive information---consistent with the prestate
non-leakiness result.
In this sense, iso\_count is a \emph{topological risk indicator}
for fine-net change, rather than a simple density proxy.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The count of embedded isolated cells is the sufficient non-leaky prestate
predictor of future fine-component change in Conway's Game of Life
and HighLife.
The response coefficient $\biso(k)$ is negative and stable across
all 7 tested horizons and all 16 conditions
(2 rules $\times$ 2 sizes $\times$ 4 density levels),
constituting an empirical temporal law.
Both iso-shuffle and target-shuffle incremental nulls are near zero
($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$), ruling out density artefacts.
The one-step mechanism is local component-context loss, not simple cell
death, and this standardised mechanism transfers across all leave-one-out
axes.
The raw response has a clean two-layer structure: a transferable
standardised direction plus a predictable amplitude $A(L,\rho)$
(LOO $R^2 = \ampLOOfull$).
Fine-net horizon tasks occupy a rank-1 coherent subspace of description
space (mean pairwise $|\cos| = \horizonCos$), while heterogeneous
prediction targets do not ($|\cos| = \selCos$), a compact geometric
diagnostic of target-indexed description privilege.

\section{Regression Details and Null Definitions}
\label{app:residual}

\subsection*{Residualisation protocol}

For each prediction world, the density nuisance covariate is
$N_{\rm live}(0)$, the total live-cell count at $t=0$
(proportional to $\rho$ up to Bernoulli sampling variance).
All regression models include $N_{\rm live}(0)$ as a linear covariate
before adding iso\_count or any fate feature.
$\Delta R^2 = R^2(\text{density + predictor}) - R^2(\text{density only})$.
The residual slope $\biso$ is the OLS coefficient on iso\_count in this
density-residualised fit.

\subsection*{Null definitions}

Two nulls are used:
(i)~\emph{iso-shuffle null}: iso\_count labels are permuted uniformly at
random within each condition; $\Delta R^2$ and slope are recomputed.
(ii)~\emph{target-shuffle null}: the target $\Ck$ is permuted; $\Delta R^2$
is recomputed with the original iso\_count.
Both converge near zero ($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$), confirming the signal
depends on the joint alignment of iso\_count with $\Ck$ and is not
a density or permutation artefact.

\section{Transition-Class Coding}
\label{app:classes}

Diagonal-adjacency class is an integer in $[1, 15]$ indexing the bit
pattern of the four diagonal neighbours of an embedded isolate
(NW, NE, SW, SE), with class $c$ equal to the binary value
$\textit{NW}\cdot 1 + \textit{NE}\cdot 2 + \textit{SW}\cdot 4 +
\textit{SE}\cdot 8$.
Class 15 (all four diagonal neighbours alive) is rare in this battery
($p \approx 0.009$, $n = 80{,}015$ instances) but contributes a negative
local response ($\bar\Delta \approx -2.52$, death fraction $= 1.0$)
comparable to other multi-diagonal classes; it is not excluded from
the analysis.
Classes 1, 2, 4, 8 are the \emph{single-diagonal} classes;
classes 3, 5, 6, 9, 10, 12 are the \emph{two-diagonal} classes;

\section{Background Observer-Scale Diagnostics}
\label{app:background}

The main text establishes the embedded-isolate response law using the
16-condition GoL/HighLife battery described in Section~\ref{sec:protocol}.
For lineage and context, this appendix records earlier observer-scale
diagnostics from the same research program.
These diagnostics show \emph{why} a target-indexed description analysis
was a natural next step: fine and coarse observers disagree systematically,
and different targets are optimally predicted at different descriptive scales.
\textbf{These figures are background diagnostics only; they are not
load-bearing evidence for the response-law estimates in the main text.}
